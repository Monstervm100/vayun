🌍 This isn't science fiction — satellite hacking really happens

Every attack you can simulate on the STARWATCH dashboard is based on things that have genuinely happened to real satellites. Below are documented incidents from public reporting, each summarized in our own words with a link to the original source so you can read the full story. Where an incident matches one of our simulator buttons, we tell you which one — so you can recreate a piece of history yourself.

Documented incidents

Ordered oldest to newest. Click any source to read the original public reporting.

2007–2008 · UNITED STATES
Hackers got within one step of commanding a NASA satellite

A U.S. government report to Congress described interference with two NASA-managed Earth-observation satellites, Landsat-7 and Terra, through a commercial ground station in Norway. For Terra, the report said the intruders "achieved all steps required to command the satellite" — they had their finger on the button — but never actually sent a command.

Simulate it on the Dashboard → 🚨 Hacker sends a fake command
Source: U.S.–China Economic and Security Review Commission, 2011 Annual Report to Congress — uscc.gov
2014 · UNITED STATES
NOAA's weather-satellite data network breached

Attackers broke into the computer network that distributes data from NOAA's weather satellites — the data behind everyday weather forecasts. NOAA had to take services offline for days while it cleaned up, briefly cutting off satellite data feeds used by forecasters worldwide.

Related demo idea → toggle "What if the server crashes?" and watch STARWATCH keep working
Source: The Washington Post, "Chinese hack U.S. weather systems, satellite network" (Nov 2014) — washingtonpost.com
2015 · GLOBAL
Spies hijacked satellite internet links to hide their tracks

Security researchers discovered that an espionage group called Turla was hijacking unencrypted commercial satellite internet downlinks. Because the satellite beams data over a huge area, the spies could quietly "borrow" other users' connections, making their command-and-control traffic almost impossible to trace.

Simulate the idea → messages from an UNKNOWN source is exactly what STARWATCH flags
Source: Kaspersky Securelist, "Satellite Turla: APT Command and Control in the Sky" — securelist.com
FEB 2022 · EUROPE
The Viasat KA-SAT attack — the biggest satellite cyberattack so far

One hour before Russia's invasion of Ukraine began, a destructive program later named "AcidRain" wiped tens of thousands of satellite-internet modems across Europe. Ukrainian military communications were disrupted, and even 5,800 wind turbines in Germany lost remote monitoring. The attackers didn't touch the satellite itself — they got in through a misconfigured VPN in the ground network, which is exactly the layer STARWATCH watches.

The lesson → the ground segment is the weakest link; monitoring every message matters
Sources: Viasat official incident overview — news.viasat.com · CISA joint advisory AA22-076A — cisa.gov
ONGOING · WORLDWIDE
GPS jamming and spoofing, happening right now

GPS is a satellite service too, and near conflict zones thousands of aircraft receive jammed or deliberately faked GPS signals every day. Researchers have documented ships appearing to "teleport" to airports they never visited. This is the real-world version of flooding or overpowering legitimate satellite signals — and you can watch it happen on a live public map (link in the box below).

Simulate it on the Dashboard → 📡 Message flood (frequency spike)
Source: C4ADS report "Above Us Only Stars: Exposing GPS Spoofing in Russia and Syria" — c4ads.org
2023 · UNITED STATES
Moonlighter — the satellite launched to be hacked

The U.S. government launched a small satellite named Moonlighter specifically so security researchers could legally attack it during the "Hack-A-Sat" competition at the DEF CON hacking conference. When an industry launches a spacecraft just for hacking practice, you know satellite cybersecurity is a serious field — the same field STARWATCH lets you explore safely in a browser.

That's this whole project → a safe, simulated satellite to practice defending
Sources: Hack-A-Sat competition — hackasat.com · The Aerospace Corporation, Moonlighter — aerospace.org

🛡️ How these hacks are prevented

Each card pairs a threat you can simulate on the dashboard with the real-world defenses satellite operators use — and shows the part STARWATCH plays: defenses try to stop attacks; monitoring (STARWATCH's job) catches whatever gets through.

Threat: fake commands
Sign and encrypt every command

Real satellites use cryptographic authentication (e.g. the CCSDS Space Data Link Security standard) so the satellite can verify each command really came from mission control — like a tamper-proof signature. Unsigned or wrongly-signed commands are simply rejected.

STARWATCH's role: flags repeated authentication failures
Threat: unknown senders
Only talk to trusted ground stations

Operators keep an allowlist of exactly which ground stations may contact each satellite, and score how much each source is trusted. Anything from an unrecognized transmitter is treated as hostile until proven otherwise ("zero trust").

STARWATCH's role: flags messages from UNKNOWN sources
Threat: message floods & jamming
Rate limits, frequency hopping, backup paths

Satellites cap how many commands they accept per hour, can hop between radio frequencies to dodge jamming, and have multiple ground stations so one blocked path doesn't mean losing the spacecraft.

STARWATCH's role: flags abnormal command frequency
Threat: ground-network break-ins
Harden the ground segment (the Viasat lesson)

The 2022 Viasat attack came through a misconfigured VPN on the ground. Defenses: keep software patched, require strong multi-factor login, and segment networks so breaking into one office PC never reaches satellite control.

STARWATCH's role: watches the ground-to-satellite boundary
Threat: brand-new, unknown attacks
AI anomaly detection + 24/7 monitoring

Rules only catch attacks you predicted. Machine-learning models (like the Isolation Forest in this project) learn what "normal" looks like and raise the alarm on anything weird — even attacks nobody has seen before.

STARWATCH's role: this IS STARWATCH — its core purpose
When prevention fails anyway
Plan for the worst: respond and recover

Operators write incident-response plans in advance: how to cut off a compromised link, switch to backup systems, and keep essential services alive. Notice how this dashboard falls back to saved data instead of a blank screen when its server "dies" — same philosophy, tiny scale.

STARWATCH's role: demo the fallback with "What if the server crashes?"
